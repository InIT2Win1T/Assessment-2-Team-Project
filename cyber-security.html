Cyber Security

 

In the current age of technology, cyber security is a major and ever evolving part of IT technology that all users, be it private, business or government need to be actively engaged with. Currently Cyber security breaches are costing billions of dollars per year to mitigate and manage. As technology is evolving, so is the toolkit being used by cyber criminals to exploit any and all types of technologies (IoT, business etc).

Ransomware

 

Ransomware has increasingly been offered as a Cybercrime-as-a-service tool, designed to encrypt all files, folders and devices on a system and locking that system down and making them inaccessible unless a random payment is made, usually in the form of a cryptocurrency, to the cyber criminals in exchange for the unlock key or code.

 

With this type of cyber threat, companies that have been unable to decrypt their data have been forced to make payment to the criminals that have locked down the systems, but this is strongly advised against. One of the reasons for this is that these criminals conduct extensive research on the companies IT infrastructure prior to deploying their ransom-ware software and often this includes locating or targeting the existing software backups that have not been isolated and once the initial ransom is paid, these backups are then used to further lockdown the system and further exploit the company or enable to company to be re-targeted at a later point in time. Phishing or spear phishing

 

Phishing is the most prevalent method used to target businesses/individuals to date. This method can be done via email, text, phone call or social media methods. What this entails is a criminal sending a seemingly legitimate message to individuals/businesses requesting an action or compromising information.

More often than not, these messages contain logoâ€™s from a legitimate source (ATO, Government etc) and legitimate branding. The point or aim of these is to acquire sensitive information (Bank account details, logins, personal identification information etc) to be used by the criminal/s for fraud.

 

Spear Phishing is a much more advanced and targeted method of this. Generally, criminals use this to target a specific individual or company with a crafted message, using tools such as social engineering to acquire more specific information about the target. As the information contained in these types of messages appears to be much more informed, accurate or relevant, the target often is tricked into opening attachments, taking the requested actions or providing the requested information. AI and its impact upon Cyber Security

 

Over the last few years, Artificial Intelligence (AI) has been used to automate more and more tasks within our personal and business lives. From servers to IoT devices, AI is being used everywhere. As this is a new technology, it has inherent risks and vulnerabilities that cyber criminals will exploit. This is due to the nature of it being a new technology. As within everything new, there are always going to be ways for it to be exploited. Unfortunately, cyber criminals are very apt at finding and exploiting new technologies.

 

With this being a new technology, the vulnerabilities or exploits that exist in this system can only be found and patched after the fact. This is how cyber criminals target this type of technology. The patches or fixes to these technologies are only applied after they have been discovered and until these are indeed discovered, if a cyber criminal/s are able to discover and exploit these prior to them being patched, they are ultimately successful in being able to exploit this.

 

What does the future look like?

 

With the evolution of cyber security and its associated threats, the future for cyber security within the IT industry will have many more evolved threats to deal with. Government based cyber warfare agencies and government sanctioned attacks are already starting to become more and more frequent. In 2010, stuxnet was used against an iranian nuclear facility triggering equipment failure. In 2017, a russian backed cyber criminal hacking group called sandworm was targeting many international organisations. Even more recently, another russian hacking group was responsible for the ransomware that targeted an american petroleum distributor as well as an multinational meat distributor. As bleak as this looks, these are examples of cyber criminals targeting multinational organisations for financial gain.

 

How will this affect individuals?

 

The effect on daily life for individuals may not seem like much at this present time, but with the evolution of exploits and tools available, combined with Cybercrime-as-a-service becoming more and more advanced, the impact upon daily life for end users as well as businesses will be the same, albeit on a different scale. While businesses will wear a higher financial toll, individuals will still suffer similarly.

From having the personal computer being locked down with ransomware, to having personal details stolen and used for cyber fraud, to being targeted from social engineering based phishing tools, the end result will still be long lasting, costly and time consuming to resolve.

This will in turn cause individuals and businesses alike to become more vigilant with their own devices and personal information as well as becoming more aware of the ways in which they can be targeted as well as becoming more aware of cyber security.
